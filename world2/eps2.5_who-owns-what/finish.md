# disconnect

The bag answers to one name now. The courier picks it up at dawn,
carries it somewhere I won't write down, and if anyone stops them,
every file tells the same small story: a courier, a cell, nothing
else. You did that with a column most users scroll past.

The ghost file bothered you. Good. It should. A number in the owner
column is the filesystem doing the only mourning it knows how to do —
faithfully, stupidly, forever — and tonight you were the one who
finally updated the record. Systems don't forget people. They just
keep pointing at the empty space with perfect accuracy until someone
with your permissions tells them to stop.

Which brings us to the word I just used.

Ownership says *whose* a file is. It doesn't say what anyone may
*do* — that's a second stamp, three letters wide, sitting right
beside the names in the same `ls -l` line you've now actually
learned to read. Read, write, execute. Owner, group, everyone else.
Nine switches that decide who starves at every door on this box.

You've been walking past a sealed door since your first night.
`/opt/apex`. Mode zero-zero-zero, my lock, my reasons. Tomorrow I
teach you the nine switches — and then I finally tell you what's
behind that door and why I never let you look.

Bring steady hands.
