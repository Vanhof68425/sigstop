# eps2.3_escaping-vim.swp

The relay came back up at 03:11. At 03:14, it delivered.

First live transmission since February. Months of reading what Reyes
left behind — files signed by someone who can't answer questions — and
then this: a voice, inside Apex, right now, asking exactly one thing.
*Prove the channel is real.*

I started the reply myself. I want that on the record. Two sentences
in, my session dropped — power, routing, I don't know which, and
"I don't know" is not a phrase I keep in stock. The channel held. The
box is fine. But I went down mid-keystroke in the one editor that
leaves a body behind when a session dies, and now there's a corpse in
the drop.

vim.

You know the jokes. Everyone knows the jokes. An industry that will
ship a trillion-parameter model into production on a Thursday
afternoon has spent twenty years laughing that it can't leave a text
editor, and nobody stops to hear what the joke confesses — that they
got lost once, in a room with the exits written on the wall, and
rather than read the wall they turned the fear into merchandise.
Comfortable. Communal. Useless. You've lived it too — closed an
entire terminal once to escape an editor. I've seen the session. We
don't have to discuss it.

Here's the room. vim has two states, and every panic in its history
was an operator who didn't know which one they were in. **Command** —
keys are orders. **Insert** — keys are text. `i` takes you from
command into insert. `Esc` takes you back. That's the door, and it
swings both ways. Leaving happens from command state: `:wq` keeps
your work and goes. `:q!` burns your changes and goes. Knowing you
can always burn is what keeps hands steady. Nothing in that room can
hold you. Nothing in that room ever held anyone.

State check. Door in, door out. Keep or burn. That's the whole
theater.

Someone is standing inside Apex holding what we need, waiting to hear
that we exist. The reply is half-written.

Finish it. Click **START**.
