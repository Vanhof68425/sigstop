# eps2.1_rm-rf_never-again.log

Their callsign was Switch.

Good instincts. Fast hands — I told you that already. Eleven jobs
without a mistake, which is the most dangerous number of jobs to have,
because that's when speed stops feeling like a risk and starts feeling
like a skill.

There's a file on this box: `switch_final_session.log`. It's the
capture from their last night. I've read it maybe two hundred times.
I keep looking for the moment it could have gone differently, and the
terrible thing is that I always find it. It's one character wide.

Read it before you touch anything else. That's not a suggestion.

Then we do the lesson, and the lesson is this: `rm` is the only
command you use that has no undo, no trash can, no second chance, and
no opinion. It will delete precisely what you tell it to, at the speed
of the disk, including the difference between `old/` and `old /`. It
is not dangerous the way a weapon is dangerous. It's dangerous the way
gravity is — indifferent, instant, and always switched on.

So operators follow three laws:

**Aim before you fire.** Never delete a pattern you haven't looked at.
`ls` the glob. `find` the pattern. See the list with your eyes, then
delete the list you saw.

**Look inside before you flatten.** `rm -rf` on a directory is a
promise that you know every file in it. If you haven't listed it,
you're not deleting a directory. You're deleting a mystery.

**Slow is smooth.** Switch saved four seconds a job for eleven jobs
and lost everything on the twelfth. Type the path. Read the path.
Then, and only then, enter.

Tonight's box has real work and real stakes, and I've arranged it the
way the world arranges it: no backup. Not because I'm cruel.

Because that's what the log is about.

Click **START**.
