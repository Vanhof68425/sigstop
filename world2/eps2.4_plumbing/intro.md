# eps2.4_plumbing.pipe

The source kept the deal. Eleven days of nothing, and then at 02:26
the relay lit up and stayed lit for forty seconds — which doesn't
sound like much until you understand what forty seconds looks like
when somebody empties their conscience through a straw.

Drop 08 is on the box. Four hundred lines and change. The rest, like
they promised: run logs, shim records, the scrub's blind spot —
folded inside Apex telemetry spray, because the only way to move it
past the wire was wrapped in the noise the wire was built to ignore.

Don't read it. I mean that operationally.

You come from a species that watches streams for a living now. Feeds.
Dashboards. Timelines. A trillion-dollar industry dedicated to data
going *past* people, and it has produced the most informed helpless
generation in history, because watching a stream is a feeling and
routing a stream is a job, and the feeling is engineered to replace
the job, and nobody notices the substitution because the feeling
updates every second and the job only pays off at the end.

Operators route. Here's the doctrine.

Every command you've ever run speaks into a channel called standard
output. Until tonight that channel pointed at your eyes. That's the
only thing that changes: where it points.

`>` aims the stream at a file. Hear this part twice: `>` **empties
the target before it writes** — silently, instantly, the way `rm`
would, without the courtesy of being famous for it. Aim it like you
learned to aim in eps2.1.

`>>` adds to the end of a file and erases nothing. The patient one.

`|` — the pipe — welds one command's mouth to another command's ear,
so the stream flows tool to tool and never touches the ground.

You already own `grep`. Tonight you'll meet `wc`, which does nothing
but count what flows through it — and counting, tonight, is how a
person on the inside of Apex finds out that nothing they risked
their life to send got dropped on the floor.

Four hundred lines. Seventeen matter. Don't find them.

Route them. Click **START**.
