# eps1.3_hidden-in-plain-sight.dot

Here's a secret that isn't one: Linux hides nothing. Not really.

Start a filename with a dot and `ls` politely doesn't mention it. That's
it. That's the whole mechanism. It was never meant for secrecy — it's
there so your home directory doesn't drown you in configuration files.
`.bashrc`. `.profile`. The machine's paperwork, filed out of sight the
way you don't display your tax returns on the mantel.

But here's what happens with a mechanism like that. Ordinary people
forget the hidden files exist. And people with something to bury start
counting on ordinary people.

Both are wrong to.

The mirror has workstation backups from four Apex engineers. Snapshots
of their home directories, taken the week the safety team walked out.
One of the four was our contact — the one from the dead drop, the
offboarded. Before leaving, they buried something for us in their own
workstation, the lazy way: behind a dot.

Which workstation? That's the point. Nobody's handing you the path this
time. You sweep all four, and the flag that shows you everything is the
one difference between you and the people who cleared these machines
for scrubbing.

Doctrine tonight is one line: **`ls` without `-a` is a summary. Never
mistake a summary for the truth.**

Click **START**.
